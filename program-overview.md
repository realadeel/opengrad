# Program Overview

## What This Is

A self-directed, AI-facilitated PhD-level program in continental philosophy. Modeled on rigorous doctoral programs at institutions like the New School, Penn State, Stony Brook, and European universities where continental philosophy is central rather than marginal.

The program provides systematic training in the history of philosophy from the ancients through contemporary critical theory, with emphasis on the continental tradition: phenomenology, existentialism, hermeneutics, critical theory, and post-structuralism.

## The Arc

### Year 1: Philosophical Foundations

Building the base. You cannot understand what continental philosophy is responding to without understanding the tradition it inherits and critiques.

**Semester 1: Ancient Philosophy**
- Plato: metaphysics, epistemology, ethics, political philosophy
- Aristotle: categories, substance, ethics, politics
- The problems they establish that philosophy never stops addressing

**Semester 2: Modern Philosophy**
- Descartes: the turn to the subject, mind-body problem, method
- Spinoza: substance monism, ethics, affect
- Hume: empiricism, causation, the self, skepticism
- Kant: the critical philosophy, conditions of possibility, limits of reason

### Year 2: The Continental Turn

The 19th century rupture. Philosophy becomes historical, embodied, suspicious of itself.

**Semester 3: German Idealism and Its Critics**
- Kant (deeper): Critique of Pure Reason, Critique of Practical Reason
- Hegel: Phenomenology of Spirit, dialectic, history, recognition
- Marx: critique of ideology, alienation, historical materialism
- Nietzsche: genealogy, will to power, critique of morality

**Semester 4: Phenomenology and Existentialism**
- Husserl: intentionality, phenomenological reduction, lifeworld
- Heidegger: Being and Time, Dasein, thrownness, authenticity
- Merleau-Ponty: embodiment, perception, the lived body
- Sartre: freedom, bad faith, being-for-others

### Year 3: Critical Theory and French Thought

The 20th century's most powerful critical traditions.

**Semester 5: The Frankfurt School**
- Adorno & Horkheimer: Dialectic of Enlightenment, critique of instrumental reason
- Benjamin: art, history, messianism
- Marcuse: One-Dimensional Man, Eros and Civilization
- Habermas: communicative reason, public sphere (and the debate with postmodernism)

**Semester 6: Post-Structuralism and Contemporary Theory**
- Foucault: power, knowledge, discourse, genealogy, biopolitics
- Deleuze: difference, immanence, desire
- Derrida: deconstruction, différance, writing
- Contemporary figures: Byung-Chul Han, Mark Fisher, Judith Butler

### Year 4: Specialization and Integration

Pursuing your own questions with the full tradition behind you.

**Semester 7: Psychoanalysis and Philosophy**
- Freud: systematic reading (metapsychology, civilization, religion)
- Lacan: the mirror stage, the three registers, desire
- Psychoanalysis and social theory: Marcuse, Žižek, critical psychology
- The critique of positivist psychology (Jacoby, your own concerns)

**Semester 8: Independent Research**
- Your book project or equivalent original work
- Drawing on the full program
- Structured writing with critical feedback

## Degree Requirements (Self-Imposed)

To "complete" this program means:

### Comprehensive Coverage
- All major figures read in primary texts
- Ability to articulate the central problems and positions of each
- Understanding of how the tradition develops and responds to itself

### Written Work
- Weekly responses throughout (500 words each)
- Unit essays (2,000-4,000 words, 4 per semester)
- Semester synthesis essays (5,000 words, end of each semester)
- Final project: book-length work or equivalent (Year 4)

Total written output: approximately 150,000-200,000 words over 4 years

### Demonstrated Competencies
- Close reading of difficult primary texts without secondary support
- Constructing rigorous philosophical arguments
- Situating thinkers within historical and intellectual context
- Applying philosophical frameworks to contemporary problems
- Writing clear, precise philosophical prose

### Examinations
At the end of Year 2 and Year 3, a comprehensive self-examination:
- Written responses to broad questions covering the material
- Evaluated critically by AI
- Must demonstrate genuine mastery, not just exposure

## Timeline

- **Per semester**: 16 weeks
- **Per year**: 2 semesters (32 weeks of active work, with breaks)
- **Total**: 4 years part-time

Weekly commitment: 10-15 hours
- Reading: 6-8 hours
- Writing: 3-4 hours
- Discussion/review: 1-2 hours

## Principles

### Primary Texts First
Always read the philosopher before reading about the philosopher. Secondary literature comes after you've formed your own interpretation.

### No AI Assistance During Reading
The encounter with the text is unmediated. Struggle with it. AI engagement comes after, for discussion and evaluation.

### Rigor Over Comfort
This program is designed to be difficult. Expect to be confused, frustrated, and challenged. That's the point.

### Writing as Thinking
You don't understand something until you can write clearly about it. The writing requirements are not assessments—they are the learning itself.

### Cumulative Development
Each semester builds on the last. By Year 3, you'll read Adorno differently because you've read Kant and Hegel. By Year 4, you'll have your own philosophical voice.
